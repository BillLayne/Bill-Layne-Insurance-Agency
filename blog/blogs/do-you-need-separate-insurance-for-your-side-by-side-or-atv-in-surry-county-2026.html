Why Your Homeowners Policy Likely Isn't Enough

Most homeowners insurance policies only provide extremely limited coverage for ATVs or side-by-sides, typically restricting protection to accidents that happen solely on your own deeded property while the vehicle is being used for "service" tasks like mowing or hauling. Once you cross your property line onto a neighbor's land, a public road, or a designated trail system, your homeowners liability usually disappears completely.

Imagine you’re riding your ATV on a trail near the Yadkin River. You take a turn a little too fast and accidentally clip another rider. If you don't have a separate off-road vehicle policy, you could be personally responsible for their medical bills and the cost to fix their machine. We aren't talking about a few hundred dollars; we're talking about tens of thousands of dollars in potential legal and medical costs.

Furthermore, homeowners insurance almost never covers "physical damage" for your ATV. This means if a tree falls on your side-by-side in the woods, or if it gets stolen while you're parked at a trailhead, your home insurance company will likely say, "Sorry, that’s not covered." A specialized policy fills these gaps, providing "comprehensive" coverage (protection against things like fire, theft, or falling objects) and "collision" coverage (protection if you hit something else).

Are you using your side-by-side for your farm? While some "farm-use" endorsements exist, they are often very specific. If you’re using that same "farm" UTV to go visit a friend down the road or head into town for a parade, you might be stepping outside of your coverage zone without even knowing it.

North Carolina Regulations and Your Off-Road Vehicle

North Carolina state law does not require insurance for off-road vehicles used strictly on private property, but you must have a liability policy if you plan to operate the vehicle on public lands, forest service roads, or if the vehicle is "street legal" and registered with the NC DMV. Even if it isn't legally mandated for your specific backyard use, the NC Rate Bureau and local agents strongly recommend a 50/100/50 liability limit to protect your financial future.

In the last few years, we’ve seen more Surry County residents seeking to make their UTVs "street legal." Under NC General Statute 20-121.1, certain "modified utility vehicles" can be operated on roads with speed limits of 35 or 55 mph (depending on local ordinances). However, to do this, you must have a title, a license plate, and insurance that meets the North Carolina minimum liability requirements (currently 30/60/25, though we recommend higher).

Even if you never plan to touch pavement, consider the "Beach Plan" or "FAIR Plan" areas of NC. While Surry County is inland, many of our neighbors haul their ATVs to the coast or the mountains. Different regions have different risks, like flooding or specialized trail requirements. A separate policy travels with you. Whether you're at Uwharrie National Forest or just riding around Elkin, your protection follows the vehicle, not just your address.

How does this impact your wallet? By having a separate policy, any claims you make for your ATV won't typically cause your homeowners insurance rates to spike. It keeps your risks separate, which is a smart way to manage your overall insurance costs in the long run.

The 4 Parts of a Great Side-by-Side Policy

A comprehensive side-by-side policy should include four key components: Bodily Injury and Property Damage Liability, Medical Payments, Comprehensive coverage, and Collision coverage. Together, these four parts ensure that both your bank account and your physical machine are protected regardless of who is at fault in an accident.

1. Liability Coverage (Your Shield)

This is the most important part. Liability coverage (the part that pays if you cause an accident) handles the costs if you hurt someone else or damage their property. Say you’re backing out of a crowded parking area at a local trail and you bump into a $40,000 custom Jeep. Without liability, that's coming out of your pocket. We recommend a minimum of $50,000 for property damage to cover the high cost of modern vehicles.

2. Medical Payments (MedPay)

Insurance can be confusing, but MedPay is simple: it pays for medical bills for you and your passengers if you're hurt while riding, regardless of who caused the wreck. Even if you have great health insurance, your deductible (the amount you pay before insurance kicks in) could be $5,000 or more. MedPay can help cover that gap immediately.

3. Comprehensive Coverage

This protects your vehicle from "acts of God" or "non-collision" events. If a freak hailstorm hits Surry County and dents your plastics, or if someone steals your ATV off your trailer at a gas station, comprehensive coverage is what pays to replace it. In 2026, with UTV prices rising, this is a must-have.

4. Collision Coverage

This is exactly what it sounds like. If you hit a tree, a fence, or another rider, collision coverage pays to repair your vehicle. Think about it: a small mistake on a slippery North Carolina clay trail could lead to thousands in frame or suspension damage. Would you rather pay a $500 deductible or $8,000 for a new front end?

Common Mistakes Surry County Riders Make

The most common mistake NC riders make is assuming their "umbrella policy" will cover an ATV accident without a primary off-road policy already in place. Umbrella policies (extra liability protection) usually require you to have a base layer of specialized insurance first; without that base, the umbrella won't "open" to protect you if a major lawsuit occurs.

Another pitfall is failing to list "custom parts and equipment." Did you add a $2,000 winch, custom wheels, or a high-end sound system? Standard policies might only cover the vehicle as it came from the factory. You need to tell your agent about these upgrades so they can be added to the policy. If you don't, and the vehicle is totaled, you'll only get the "stock" value back.

Lastly, don't forget about "Uninsured Motorist" coverage. Just because you are responsible doesn't mean the person who hits you on the trail will be. If an uninsured rider crashes into you and leaves you with medical bills, your own policy's Uninsured Motorist coverage can step in to pay for your damages. It’s a vital safety net in a state where off-road insurance isn't always mandatory.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Does my NC auto insurance cover my ATV if it’s on a trailer?

A: Your auto insurance will typically cover the liability if the trailer hits someone while attached to your truck, but it will not cover the physical damage to the ATV itself. If the ATV falls off or is damaged in a wreck, you need a separate off-road policy with collision coverage to pay for the repairs.

Q: Is ATV insurance required by law in North Carolina?

A: Insurance is not legally required for ATVs used strictly on private land; however, it is required for any vehicle registered for street use or operated on certain public lands and trails. Regardless of the law, liability insurance is highly recommended to protect your personal assets from lawsuits.

Q: Can I get a discount if I only use my side-by-side for farming?

A: Yes, many carriers offer specialized rates or endorsements for "utility" use versus "recreational" use. If your vehicle is primarily a tool for your Surry County farm, let your agent know so they can apply the correct classification and potentially lower your rate.

Q: What happens if someone else is driving my ATV and gets in an accident?

A: In most cases, insurance follows the vehicle, meaning your policy would still provide coverage if you gave the person permission to ride. However, some "budget" policies exclude guest riders, so it is vital to review your specific policy language with an expert agent.

Q: Does side-by-side insurance cover theft from my backyard?

A: Yes, if you have "comprehensive" coverage on your policy, it will pay to replace your vehicle if it is stolen from your property, a trailer, or a trailhead. Homeowners insurance often limits or denies coverage for motorized vehicles, making this specific coverage essential.
