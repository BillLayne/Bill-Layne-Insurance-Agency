What Is a Mutual Insurer — and Why Does It Matter?

Most insurance companies you see advertised — the gecko, the woman in the white suit, the duck — are stock companies. They have shareholders. Those shareholders expect dividends. Every quarter, profits get pulled out to keep those shareholders happy.

A mutual insurer is different. There are no stockholders. The policyholders are the owners of the company. When NC Grange Mutual has a good year, those profits don't get paid out to Wall Street — they go back into the company's reserves to protect against the next big hurricane, ice storm, or hailstorm.

Put plainly: a mutual operates more like a cooperative or a partnership. Every policyholder is in the same boat together. The interests of the company and the interests of the policyholder are pointed in the same direction.

That's not marketing language. That's the actual legal structure NC Grange Mutual has had since the State of North Carolina licensed them in 1934. In their 90+ years of service, NC Grange Mutual has only ever had to assess policyholders one time — after Hurricane Hazel in 1954, before the company began carrying reinsurance. Since 1955, reinsurance has protected both policyholders and company surplus against catastrophic loss.

A mutual insurer's profits go back into the company, not out to shareholders. That structure has kept NC Grange Mutual quietly strong through every NC hurricane, ice storm, and recession of the last 90 years.

Step 2: An independent adjuster is assigned. This is the key difference. NC Grange Mutual uses experienced, local independent adjusters — not employees stationed in a call center five states away. Your adjuster is typically based in your area and has handled the specific type of loss you've had, whether that's a tree on a roof, a kitchen fire, hail damage, or barn damage.

Step 3: Protect property from further damage. Tarp the roof. Move what you can. Keep receipts for any temporary repairs. NC Grange Mutual specifically asks you to take reasonable steps to prevent additional loss.

Step 4: Be ready with documentation. Policy number, date of loss, address, a comprehensive list of damaged or stolen items, the cause of damage, and a police report number if theft is involved. The more organized you are up front, the smoother the claim goes.
