What Are the New NC 50/100/50 Limits for Mount Airy Drivers?

This is the single biggest change to NC auto insurance in years, and every Mount Airy driver renewing in 2025 or 2026 is feeling it. Here's the plain-English breakdown.

Effective on every policy renewing on or after July 1, 2025, North Carolina now requires 50/100/50 minimum liability limits — that's:

• $50,000 bodily injury per person
• $100,000 bodily injury per accident
• $50,000 property damage per accident

That replaces the old 30/60/25 minimums most folks carried for decades. On top of that, every NC policy now must include uninsured/underinsured motorist (UM/UIM) coverage — no more skipping it to save a few bucks. The NC Department of Insurance has the full rule if you want the official version.

For Mount Airy drivers, this matters in three ways. First, you're carrying more liability protection than ever — which is good news if you're ever at fault in a serious crash. Second, the higher limits drive a roughly 5% statewide rate increase that's phasing in across NC. Third, mandatory UM/UIM matters more in rural Surry County than in big cities, because uninsured driver rates trend higher in rural counties — that mandatory coverage is genuinely protecting you.

The new 50/100/50 limits are not optional. If your renewal already happened, you're carrying them whether you noticed or not — and so is every other Mount Airy driver. The smart move is offsetting the cost, not fighting the rule.

This is the question every Mount Airy driver Googles at midnight after a renewal notice arrives. Here's the honest answer for 2026.

What moves your number up or down:

• Driver age and experience. A 22-year-old commuter pays meaningfully more than a 55-year-old with a clean record.
• Vehicle. A new SUV with a $40K replacement value insures very differently than a paid-off pickup.
• Driving record. One at-fault accident or DUI can swing your rate 30–80% for years.
• Annual mileage. I-77 commuters to Winston-Salem typically pay more than someone who works close to home.
• Credit-based insurance score. NC carriers use this — a small improvement can mean real savings.
• Coverage choices. Liability-only is cheap. Full coverage with low deductibles costs more (but protects more).

Statewide, NC is seeing a roughly 5% rate increase phasing in through 2026 driven by the new limits and rising claim costs. Around home in Surry County, that's playing out as a $60–$120 annual bump for most drivers — not catastrophic, but absolutely worth fighting with a good comparison.
