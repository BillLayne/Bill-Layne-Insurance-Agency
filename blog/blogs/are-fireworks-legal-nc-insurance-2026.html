What Fireworks Are Actually Legal in North Carolina?

North Carolina is one of the strictest states in the country on consumer fireworks. The simple rule of thumb our local fire departments use: if it explodes, spins, leaves the ground, or flies through the air, it's illegal here. That's spelled out in the state's fireworks statutes (NC General Statutes Chapter 14, Article 54).

So what can you legally light up in your Elkin backyard? The non-explosive, ground-based stuff:

Legal in NC: wire sparklers, fountains, snakes and glow worms, smoke devices, party poppers, snappers, and string poppers — novelty items that stay put and don't take flight.

Illegal in NC: firecrackers, bottle rockets, Roman candles, ground spinners, mortars, and any aerial or exploding device. Using or possessing these is a Class 2 misdemeanor, with penalties that can reach a $500 fine and jail time. And there's no holiday exception — the same rules apply on the Fourth of July as any other day of the year.

One more detail Surry County parents ask about every June: children under 16 can't legally buy or use fireworks in North Carolina — not even sparklers, which burn hot enough to cause serious burns.

In North Carolina, anything that explodes or leaves the ground is illegal — statewide, every day of the year, even on your own property with permission.

The South Carolina Border Trap — and Why It Matters Here

Every June, families across the NC foothills make the familiar run down I-77 to the big fireworks superstores just over the South Carolina line. South Carolina sells the whole arsenal — mortars, Roman candles, the works. It's legal to buy there. The problem is what happens when you drive back across into North Carolina.

Here's the part that trips people up: buying fireworks legally in South Carolina does not make them legal to use in North Carolina. The second those aerials cross the state line into Surry, Yadkin, or Wilkes County, they're contraband — and lighting them in your Jonesville or Dobson driveway is the same Class 2 misdemeanor as if you'd bought them off the back of a truck.

Why does an insurance agent care where you bought your fireworks? Because that single fact — legal or illegal — is what decides whether a claim gets paid. We'll get to exactly why in the next section.

There's been talk in Raleigh for years about legalizing consumer fireworks, and a bill surfaces almost every session. But those efforts keep stalling — opposed by a coalition of firefighters and fire-safety groups, with the NC Department of Insurance and the State Fire Marshal's office historically lined up against them. As of summer 2026, nothing has changed: the restrictive law still stands.

A South Carolina receipt won't help you in North Carolina. Once illegal fireworks cross the line into Surry County, using them is against the law — and that has real insurance consequences.

Does Homeowners Insurance Cover Fireworks Damage in NC?

Usually yes — if the fireworks were legal. A standard North Carolina homeowners policy generally treats a fireworks fire like any other accidental fire, and three parts of your policy can come into play:

Dwelling and Other Structures cover damage to your house, detached garage, or shed if a legal firework sparks a fire. Personal Property covers your belongings inside. And Personal Liability — commonly $100,000 to $500,000 — steps in if your fireworks injure a guest or damage a neighbor's property, paying their medical bills and any legal costs up to your limit.

Now the catch that makes this a North Carolina story specifically. Most homeowners policies exclude damage caused by illegal acts, and using fireworks that are banned in your state is exactly that. So if those South Carolina bottle rockets land in the gutter, catch the pine straw, and take part of the roof with them, the insurer can deny the claim outright — leaving you to pay the full repair, plus a possible fine, out of pocket.

It's worth remembering a deductible applies to any covered fire loss, too — typically $500 to $1,000 on the dwelling side. (If you want the full breakdown on how deductibles work, we covered that in detail in our NC deductible guide.) For the official coverage basics, the Insurance Information Institute is a solid neutral resource, and the NC Department of Insurance publishes consumer guidance for North Carolina specifically.

Legal fireworks that cause a fire are typically covered like any accidental fire. Illegal fireworks fall under the illegal-acts exclusion — which is how a fun night turns into a denied claim.

What Your Insurance Won't Cover

Even with perfectly legal fireworks, a few gaps surprise people. Knowing them ahead of the cookout saves a lot of heartburn:

Your own injuries. Homeowners liability covers other people — guests, neighbors — not you or anyone in your household. If you or your child gets burned, that's a job for your health insurance, not your home policy.

Your own car. If a stray firework scorches your truck in the driveway, that's not a homeowners claim — it falls under the comprehensive portion of your auto policy, subject to your auto deductible.

Anything intentional or grossly negligent. Aiming a Roman candle at the neighbor's barn as a joke isn't an "accident" any insurer will pay for. Intentional damage is excluded across the board.

Here in the Yadkin Valley and across Surry County, the good news is that most of these gaps are easy to plan around once you know they exist — and a couple of them are exactly where a personal umbrella policy earns its keep.

Your home policy covers harm to others, not to you, your household, or your own vehicle. Those fall to health and auto coverage instead.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are fireworks legal in North Carolina?

Most are not. North Carolina only allows ground-based, non-explosive items like sparklers, fountains, snakes, smoke devices, and party poppers. Anything that explodes, spins, leaves the ground, or flies — firecrackers, bottle rockets, Roman candles, and mortars — is illegal statewide, even on your own property with permission.

Does homeowners insurance cover fireworks damage in NC?

Usually yes, if the fireworks were legal. A standard NC homeowners policy can cover fire damage to your dwelling, other structures, and belongings, plus liability if your fireworks injure a guest. But most policies exclude damage from illegal fireworks, so a fire started by banned aerials can lead to a denied claim.

Are fireworks bought in South Carolina legal to use in NC?

No. Fireworks that are illegal in North Carolina stay illegal here even if you bought them legally across the line in South Carolina. Driving them home does not make them legal, and using them can still void your homeowners coverage if they cause a fire or injury.

Does my insurance cover my own fireworks injuries?

No. Your homeowners liability covers injuries to other people, not to you or members of your household — those medical bills fall to your health insurance. If a stray firework damages your own car, that is handled by the comprehensive portion of your auto policy, subject to your deductible.

What happens if a neighbor's firework damages my home or car in NC?

If a neighbor is legally responsible, the liability portion of their homeowners policy may pay for damage to your home or car and any injuries. If they have no coverage or are not found at fault, your own homeowners or comprehensive auto coverage may apply, subject to your deductible. Document everything with photos.
