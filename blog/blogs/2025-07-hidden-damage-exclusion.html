Why Didn't My Insurance Pay? Understanding NC's 'Hidden Damage' Exclusion

If you're a North Carolina homeowner whose claim was denied for "gradual damage" or "hidden deterioration," you're not alone. I'll explain exactly what this means, why it happens, and how to protect yourself from costly surprises in the future.

What is hidden damage in homeowners insurance?

Hidden damage in homeowners insurance is gradual deterioration or damage that occurs slowly over time, typically from wear and tear, lack of maintenance, or long-term exposure to elements. Insurance companies exclude this because policies cover sudden and accidental damage, not predictable deterioration that homeowners should maintain.

Here in North Carolina, we see this exclusion applied frequently, especially after our humid summers and unpredictable weather patterns. Understanding this exclusion can save you thousands of dollars and prevent claim denials.

What damage is not covered by homeowners insurance?

Homeowners insurance typically doesn't cover gradual damage, maintenance issues, wear and tear, pest infestations, mold from long-term moisture, foundation settling, and damage from flooding or earthquakes. These exclusions apply because they're either predictable, preventable through maintenance, or require separate policies.

Understanding these exclusions is critical for North Carolina homeowners. Our state's weather patterns and humidity levels make certain types of damage more common, and knowing what's excluded helps you prepare.

Complete List of Common Exclusions

  • Wear, tear, and deterioration
  • Rust, corrosion, and decay
  • Settling and cracking of foundations
  • Damage from pests (termites, rodents)
  • Mold, fungus, or wet/dry rot
  • Mechanical breakdown of systems
  • Faulty workmanship or materials
  • Neglect or improper maintenance
  • Flooding (requires separate policy)
  • Earth movement/earthquakes

Common Questions About Hidden Damage Claims in NC

Sudden water damage happens quickly from a specific event, like a pipe bursting or washing machine hose breaking. Gradual damage occurs slowly over time, like a small leak that drips for months. Insurance covers sudden damage because it's unexpected and accidental, while gradual damage is considered a maintenance issue you should have caught and fixed.

In North Carolina, you should report damage "promptly" or "as soon as reasonably possible." While there's no specific deadline, waiting more than 30 days can lead to denial. I recommend calling within 24-48 hours of discovering damage. The longer you wait, the harder it becomes to prove the damage was sudden rather than gradual.

Generally, denied claims shouldn't increase your rates in North Carolina. However, the claim still goes on your CLUE report and multiple claims (even denied ones) within 3-5 years can affect your insurability. Some companies view frequent claims as a red flag, regardless of payout. That's why having a local agent review damage before filing can be valuable.

If you discover old water damage, document it immediately with photos and get professional assessments to determine the age and cause. Don't try to file a claim for old damage as new – that's fraud. Instead, fix the damage and prevent future issues. Consider having a contractor write a detailed report about the damage timeline for your records.

Mold coverage depends on the cause. If mold results from a covered peril (like sudden pipe burst), it's typically covered. But mold from long-term hidden leaks, high humidity, or poor ventilation is excluded. In NC's humid climate, mold prevention through proper ventilation and moisture control is essential. Some policies offer limited mold coverage – check your specific policy.

Public adjusters can help with complex claims, but they typically charge 10-20% of your settlement. For hidden damage denials, I'd first try appealing yourself or with your agent's help. If that fails, then consider a public adjuster. In NC, ensure they're licensed through the Department of Insurance. Sometimes a simple contractor's report is more cost-effective than hiring an adjuster.

Keep all receipts for home maintenance, including HVAC service, roof repairs, plumbing work, and pest inspections. Take dated photos during any repairs or inspections. Maintain a home maintenance log noting when you cleaned gutters, checked for leaks, or noticed any issues. These records prove you're a responsible homeowner and can be crucial in fighting claim denials.
